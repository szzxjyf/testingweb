Before I go into the ATP proposal, let me briefly explain what the FMDB business is and how it generates value.
China's commodity futures market mainly consists of four exchanges: SHFE, DCE, ZCE and GFE. There are three main types of market participants: domestic investors, international clients and Qualified Foreign Investors (QFIs). HSBC's FMDB business primarily supports international clients and QFIs trading in this market.
The business model is relatively straightforward. Investors place margin with futures brokers, and those margin funds are ultimately deposited with the exchanges. As an approved Futures Margin Depository Bank, HSBC holds these margin deposits on behalf of the exchanges, generating stable deposit balances and net interest income.
Back in 2019, thanks to Berenger's efforts, HSBC obtained the FMDB licence for international clients trading specific products on DCE, establishing our initial presence in this market.
More recently, around two to three weeks ago, both DCE and CZCE visited HSBC and encouraged us to apply for the FMDB licence. Following a series of internal discussions, we believe this is an opportunity worth pursuing, not only from a business perspective but also to further strengthen our relationship with the exchanges. We've already presented the proposal to John and Mr. Lin, and the project will require close collaboration with MSS.
The estimated total project cost is around USD 200K. Our proposal assumes MSS will co-fund half of the investment, resulting in a funding ask of USD 100K from our side. From a business perspective, we believe the combination of DCE and CZCE creates a much stronger value proposition than either exchange alone. It significantly expands our product coverage for international clients and, more importantly, serves as another strategic stepping stone in building HSBC's long-term FMDB footprint across China's commodity futures market.


Our second proposal is the Telegram Fraud Detection initiative. Unlike the first proposal, this project is primarily driven by regulatory requirements rather than commercial opportunities.
Today, we already have multiple fraud screening controls in place throughout the RTP payment journey. Before a payment enters online banking, it is screened by our SAS Fraud Management system. Later in the process, before funds are released, we perform additional screening against the WOLF watchlist.
However, under the latest regulatory expectations in China, there is still a control gap. Banks are expected to proactively detect suspicious transaction patterns, rather than relying solely on pre-defined rules or watchlists.
This proposal addresses that gap. By analysing transaction behaviour—such as unusual same-day "rapid in and rapid out" fund movements—the system can generate alerts for potentially fraudulent activities, enabling earlier intervention and helping prevent telecom fraud.
The total project cost is estimated at USD 1.568 million. The investment will be shared equally among BBC, IB and GPS, with each business contributing approximately USD 523K.


Our third proposal focuses on improving the payment experience for our customers.
Today, HSBC supports a maximum of 30 characters for both the beneficiary name and payment details when initiating payments through HSBCnet and Connect. However, the market standard has already moved to 60 characters.
This limitation creates two practical issues. First, when the beneficiary's name exceeds 30 characters, customers often have to place the remaining part of the name in the payment details field. In some cases, this can lead to payment rejections because the beneficiary information is incomplete or inconsistent. Second, customers cannot provide sufficiently detailed payment information when more than 30 characters are required, which may create operational risks, particularly for payments with strict remittance information requirements.
Our proposal is therefore to increase the character limit from 30 to 60 for both the beneficiary name and payment details. The solution has been assessed as technically feasible.
The total funding required is approximately USD 510K, comprising USD 300K for HSBCnet and USD 210K for Connect. As the enhancement will be developed jointly with Hang Seng Bank, there is also an opportunity to share the benefits across both organisations.
Overall, the investment delivers two key benefits: it aligns HSBC with current market practice and helps reduce payment returns, ultimately improving the overall customer experience.
